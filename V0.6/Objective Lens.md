# Objective Lens

The objective lens, a plano-convex lens placed near an object to manipulate light in such a way that the focal point is short. The distance between the focal point and the optical centre of the lens determines the magnification of the image seen. 
The shorter the distance, the higher the magnification. 
The radius of the lens is small, in order to maximise magnification using the least material and fluid. 

## Plano-Convex Lens
Plano-convex lens is a type of optical lens that is curved on one side and flat on the other. This type of lens is used to minimize spherical aberration. Spherical aberration is a phenomenon in which the outer parts of a lens do not bring light rays into the same focus as the central part. This phenomena occurs when using a lens with curvature being a part of a sphere. Subjectively, it appears as warps in the image in a "stretching" fashion. Plano-convex lenses can be imagined as a convex and a concave lens stacked against each other into one object. The concave lens will correct the spherical abberation produced by the convex lens.

## Flexible Membrane

The flexible membrane (Fmembrane) is made of transparent, flexible material. It can be a silicon sheet, but due to accesibility, acrylic tape is used instead. The stickiness is reduced by washing using soap and water (process will be referred to as "treatment"). 
One face is left untreated, to hold the spacer in place without using CA glue. 
The Fmembrane is supported by steel washers (for bolt and nut). 

### Making Flexible Membrane

1. The acrylic tape is stretched then wraped around the steel washer. The hole at the center of the washer allows a circular geometry when the Fmembrane expands and bulges. 

## Spacer

The spacer is made of polymers like PET. 

### Making Spacer

1. A hole is punched using a regular hole puncher as it happens to punch a hole with just the right diameter (Cutting by hand is also another method, choose the best method that suits your case). This allows the liquid inside of the lens to be held in place when the syringe is not applying any pressure. 
2. A slit is cut into the PET spacer to allow the syringe needle to be placed without interfering with membrane of either side (Fmembrane & IFmembrane). 
3. A circular shape matching that of the washer is cut out of the spacer to make a more flushed appearance. (Optional)
4. Both faces of the IFMembrane are sanded into a rough surface for better adhesion of CA glue.

## Inflexible Membrane

The inflexible membrane (IFmembrane) is made of transparent, rigid material. A flat, transparent PET sheet (mouthwash bottle) is chosen as the material as it is optically clear enough to make a lens out of, and is rigid enough to withstand what little pressure the syringe can exert on it. It also doesn't suffer from poor CA glue adhesion, like other polymers do.

### Making Inflexible Membrane

The circular membrane with diameter matching that of the washer is cut out of the PET sheet. 

## Assembly of Objective Lens

**Tip#1: When applying CA glue and baking soda, avoid optically critical parts like axially centre part of the entire contraption. Any fogging or stuck baking soda cannot be removed, resulting in the entire process of part preparation and assembly repeated (from gruesome experience).**
**Tip#2: Blow off excess baking soda to prevent baking soda from sticking to Fmembrane. Avoid wiping on Fmembrane as fingerprints might be left behind and ruin the optical experience.**

1. Three very small drops of CA glue is applied to one side of the spacer. 
2. CA glue is smeared throughout the entire surface using anything that will not stick easily to CA glue. Examples of substances that stick well to CA glue are fingers and paper. The smeared CA glue should appear to be a very thin layer of liquid that barely covers the entire surface of the spacer. 
3. The IFmembrane is placed on top of the spacer, then pressed together to ensure full contact with the glue. If any amount of glue is spread into the center hole, no matter how minuscule the amount is, the excess glue is immediately absorbed away using paper to prevent permanent fogging that fatally obscures the clarity of the lens. 
4. After 5 minutes or so, composition is flipped so that the circumference is accessed easily. 
5. Apply CA glue to edges where spacer and inflexible membrane meet, to seal off the seam. Baking soda may be applied after 5 minute of curing, to slightly speed up the curing process. Immediately applying CA glue will cause the cured glue to be brittle and susceptible to pressure. 
6. With IFmembrane facing upward, the composition is placed on top of the Fmembrane face completely covered in acrylic tape.
7. The composition is pressed together to ensure there is no visible seam. Remove pressure and check for splitting at the face where they meet. If there is splitting, The pressing process is repeated until there is no split occurring.
8. CA glue is applied at the edge where the two faces meet, acting as a seal. Avoid the slit made in the spacer. Small amounts of baking soda may be applied after 5-10 minutes of curing, to slightly speed up the curing process. Immediately applying CA glue will cause the cured glue to be brittle and susceptible to pressure.
9. A de-sharpened syringe needle is inserted into the slit with care to avoid damaging the Fmembrane. The mouth of the needle should be placed not far from the walls of the spacer. Can be positioned within the slit, but will compromise structural integrity. Position ultimately depends on maker's priority: bubble removal or rigidity of composition.
10. CA glue is applied at where the needle is inserted. Monitor the CA glue creeping inside the slit, NEVER let it reach the chamber on the contraption. Baking soda may be applied after 5 minute of curing, to slightly speed up the curing process. Repeat process at least twice, but don't let the solid grow too thick.
11. A 1ml syringe is cut around the 0.2ml mark. The plunger is also vut at the length suitable with the syringe body. The cut syringe is attached to the inserted needle. Water is carefully introduced into the composition, ensuring air bubbles are removed as much as possible to maintain an uninterrupted image.
12. Trapped air inside syringe should also be removed, before the syringe is reattached to the needle.

## Testing & Calibration

**Note: Neutral position is the position of syringe piston where there is no curvature of the Fmembrane. When looking through lens, no magnification shall be observed**
- Slowly increase pressure by compressing the syringe past the neutral position. Observe when the Fmembrane is stretched to its limit and about to burst. Quickly mark the level of compression applied on the syringe piston, then quickly decompress the syringe piston. The mark will now be the maximum level of compression one should apply. 
- Slowly decrease pressure by decompressing the syringe past the neutral position. Observe when the Fmembrane curve inward to such an extend it touches the IFmembrane. Mark the level of compression. That is the maximum level of decompression one should apply. Any further decompression will force Fmembrane against IFmembrane, producing counter-productive imagery effects. Prolonged contact between Fmembrane and IFmembrane can lead to Fmembrane adhering to IFmembrane. Return compression level to neutral.
